Does a bearing model transfer to a rig it has never seen?

Part 1 taught a model to predict failures on one test rig. The next question: does any of that survive meeting a machine it has never seen? It's the difference between knowing when your own car sounds sick and diagnosing a stranger's truck. Every rig colors vibration differently and lifetimes span 100× — 37 minutes to 45 days — so this study pools three public run-to-failure datasets (NASA IMS roller bearings, XJTU-SY and FEMTO ball bearings, 36 lifetimes), converts remaining life into a fuel-gauge-style life fraction, and measures the generalization gap directly: how much worse does the model get when the entire rig — not just the bearing — is hidden from training?

Method

Make three rigs speak one language, then hide one.

Step 1 · One tableLife fraction, not hours

"X hours left" can't cross rigs — one bearing lives 45 days, another 37 minutes, so absolute hours mostly reveal which dataset you're on. Instead the target is a fuel gauge: remaining useful life (RUL) as a fraction of total life, 1 → 0. Every feature is also divided by that bearing's own healthy baseline, so 2.0 means "twice its healthy hum" on any machine. All three datasets land in one shared table; a fourth rig later is a drop-in loader.

Step 2 · Physics featuresEnvelope spectrum from geometry

A defect drums. Every ball rolling over a crack makes a tiny impact, and the drumbeat's tempo (BPFO, BPFI, BSF) is computable straight from the bearing drawing — geometry tells you which rhythm outer-race, inner-race and roller damage will play. Envelope-spectrum features listen for exactly those rhythms. Because they come from geometry, not from any particular rig, they're the features most likely to transfer — and they were proven on a known failure before being trusted.

Step 3 · Honest labelsTrain only after onset

A healthy bearing's hum says nothing about when it will die — the crack that kills it doesn't exist yet. So the model is an oncologist, not a fortune teller: the 6σ alarm from Part 1 (vibration leaving the bearing's own healthy band) decides when a case file is opened, and the random forest trains and predicts only after that onset.

Step 4 · The examLOBO vs LODO

Two exams. Leave-one-bearing-out (LOBO): the model has seen this rig's siblings, just never this bearing — diagnosing a new car of a familiar model. Leave-one-dataset-out (LODO): the whole rig is the stranger's truck. The difference between the two scores is the generalization gap — the number this study exists to measure.

Results

The gap is small. The reasons are interesting.

First, prove the physics feature works

A unit test, but for physics. IMS test 2 bearing 1 died of a documented outer-race defect, so its envelope spectrum must drum at the computed outer-race tempo (BPFO, 236 Hz) and its harmonics — and it does, sharply, while the inner-race and roller rhythms stay silent. The drumbeat's energy grows 21.7× over the test. Only after this check did envelope features enter the model.

The headline: bearing unseen vs whole rig unseen

The headline: hiding the entire rig barely moves the median error (0.186 → 0.173). Two surprises. The roller-vs-ball wall we expected never showed up — models trained only on ball bearings handled the roller rig fine (0.166). And the hardest dataset either way is FEMTO, for a mundane reason: its snapshots last 0.1 seconds, so every diagnosis is made from a finger-snap of audio. Recording quality beat bearing-type physics as the dominant difficulty.

Best, median — and the worst, on purpose

Predicted vs true life fraction; the shaded band is where 90% of the forest's 300 trees land. Two things explain what you see. First, the model has no memory — every snapshot gets a fresh diagnosis, like a doctor working from a single blood test with amnesia in between; that's the jitter. Second, mid-life is genuinely ambiguous ("somewhat sick" looks the same at 30%, 50% and 70% of life) while the end is unmistakable — so the band is wide in the middle and snaps shut at the end. The best case is partly an easy exam: its alarm fired at 93% of life, leaving only the final, obvious stretch to track. The worst case is the honest one: alarm at 16%, a long ambiguous middle age, and the model called it nearly dead throughout — a full miss. Medians hide bearings like this; the report doesn't.

Population model vs curve extrapolation — no winner declared

Literature says curve extrapolation fails early and shines late, so the population model should win early and hand over the baton. Our data refused the clean story — because the extrapolator is a student who only answers the questions it's sure about: it only forecasts when damage is actually growing exponentially (17–33% of early snapshots), so its flattering early score is graded on a self-picked easy subset. The forest answers every question, with U-shaped error (worst right after onset). With 36 lifetimes we report the curves and the coverage instead of declaring a crossover.

Leakage guard: is it secretly identifying datasets?

The cheating check. The easiest way to look smart here is to recognize which dataset a snapshot came from — like grading exam papers by recognizing the handwriting — and the obvious fingerprint is wall-clock degradation speed, which differs 100× between rigs. Those rate features carry 0.3% of total importance: the forest leans on smoothed RMS (27%), smoothed kurtosis, and a broad spread of geometry-derived envelope bands. Damage signals, not dataset IDs. Two ablations complete the audit: removing the roller-bearing data hurts ball-bearing predictions (0.192 → 0.205 — the odd rig out was helping), and excluding cage-failure bearings changes nothing (0.188 both ways).

Honest limitations

Read these before quoting the numbers.

  • "Failure time" is a chosen convention, not a fact of nature: IMS ends when the rig stopped itself; XJTU and FEMTO end at the first 20 g peak (XJTU's own criterion, extended to FEMTO — whose failure definition the literature genuinely argues about). Every label is conditional on that choice.
  • A life fraction is only knowable after the death. Translating errors into hours uses the true total life — hindsight no live system has. The hour figures are for intuition, not a promise.
  • The 6σ alarm is the gatekeeper: where it fires early (XJTU bearings drift while warming up), near-healthy data sneaks into training; where it fires at 95% of life, the model gets a tiny, easy exam. The detector, not the forest, defines the model's jurisdiction.
  • FEMTO's 0.1 s finger-snap snapshots give blurry 10 Hz frequency resolution against 100–170 Hz fault rhythms, and its NSK 6804 geometry is a literature value, not a verified drawing. FEMTO being hardest is partly a statement about its recordings, not its bearings.
  • 36 lifetimes is small. That's why there is deliberately no deep learning here (it would memorize rigs, not physics — a scoping decision, not a gap), and why medians, IQRs and worst cases are reported instead of single averages.
  • All three rigs run constant, accelerated load. Real machines change speed and load, which moves every feature with zero damage present. Nothing here tests that.
