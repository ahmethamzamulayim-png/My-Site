Does a bearing model transfer to a rig it has never seen?

Part 1 predicted failures on one test rig. The open research question is whether any of that transfers: every rig colors the vibration differently, and lifetimes span 100× — from 37 minutes to 45 days. This study pools three public run-to-failure datasets (NASA IMS roller bearings, XJTU-SY and FEMTO ball bearings, 36 lifetimes total), converts remaining life to a dimensionless life fraction, and measures the generalization gap directly: how much worse does a model get when the entire test rig — not just the bearing — is held out of training?

Method

Make three rigs speak one language, then hide one.

Step 1 · One tableLife fraction, not hours

Absolute-hour labels would teach the model to identify datasets (a FEMTO bearing never has 40 days left). The target becomes remaining life fraction (1 → 0), every feature is normalized to that bearing's own healthy baseline, and all three datasets land in one table with a shared schema — adding a fourth rig later is a drop-in loader.

Step 2 · Physics featuresEnvelope spectrum from geometry

Each defect type impacts at a frequency computable from bearing geometry (BPFO, BPFI, BSF). Band energies in the Hilbert-envelope spectrum at those frequencies are the most rig-transferable features available — and they were validated on a known failure before use, not assumed to work.

Step 3 · Honest labelsTrain only after onset

Healthy vibration carries no information about the future, and in fraction space no single label cap exists (the healthy phase is 30–90% of life depending on the bearing). So the 6σ detector from Part 1 defines the model's jurisdiction: the forest trains and predicts only after degradation onset.

Step 4 · The examLOBO vs LODO

Leave-one-bearing-out (LOBO): the model has seen the rig, not the bearing. Leave-one-dataset-out (LODO): the entire rig is new. The difference between those two scores is the generalization gap — the single number this study exists to measure.

Results

The gap is small. The reasons are interesting.

First, prove the physics feature works

Unit test for physics: IMS test 2 bearing 1 died of a documented outer-race defect, so its envelope spectrum must peak at the computed outer-race frequency (236 Hz) and harmonics — and it does, sharply, while inner-race and roller bands stay quiet. The band energy rises 21.7× over the test. Only after this check did envelope features enter the model.

The headline: bearing unseen vs whole rig unseen

Median error barely moves when the whole rig is hidden (0.186 → 0.173). Two surprises: the expected roller-vs-ball wall never appeared — IMS held-out scored a middling 0.166 — and the hardest dataset both ways is FEMTO, whose 0.1-second snapshots are simply the noisiest data. Data quality beat bearing-type physics as the dominant difficulty.

Best, median — and the worst, on purpose

Predicted vs true life fraction with 5–95% forest-spread bands. Best case locks on. The median case (a FEMTO bearing) tracks the trend through heavy noise. The worst case is shown because it is the honest one: onset fired at 16% of life, degradation then crawled, and the model called the bearing nearly dead the whole time — a full trajectory miss. Medians hide bearings like this; the report doesn't.

Population model vs curve extrapolation — no winner declared

Literature says curve extrapolation fails early and shines late. Our data refused the clean story: the extrapolator only produces a forecast when an exponential actually fits — 17–33% of early snapshots — so its flattering early error is graded on a self-selected easy subset. The forest answers always, with U-shaped error (worst right after onset). With 36 lifetimes we report the curves and the coverage instead of declaring a crossover.

Leakage guard: is it secretly identifying datasets?

The suspicious features — degradation rates in absolute per-hour units, which differ 100× between rigs and could fingerprint the dataset — carry 0.3% of total importance. The forest leans on smoothed RMS (27%), smoothed kurtosis, and a broad spread of geometry-derived envelope bands: damage signals, not dataset IDs. Two ablations complete the picture: removing the roller-bearing data hurts ball-bearing predictions (0.192 → 0.205, mild positive transfer), and excluding cage-failure bearings changes nothing (0.188 both ways).

Honest limitations

Read these before quoting the numbers.

  • "Failure time" is a chosen convention, not a fact: IMS ends when the rig stopped itself; XJTU and FEMTO end at the first 20 g peak (XJTU's published criterion, extended to FEMTO — whose failure definition is genuinely debated in the literature). Every label is conditional on that choice.
  • Life fraction is only knowable in hindsight. Hour-translations of the errors use the true total life — information no live system would have.
  • The 6σ onset detector gates everything: where it fires early (XJTU bearings have a warm-up drift), near-healthy data enters training; the model's jurisdiction is defined by the detector, not by the forest.
  • FEMTO's 0.1 s snapshots give 10 Hz envelope resolution against 100–170 Hz fault frequencies, and its NSK 6804 geometry is a literature assumption, not a verified drawing. FEMTO being the hardest dataset is partly a data-quality statement.
  • 36 lifetimes is small. That's why there is deliberately no deep learning here (it would memorize rigs, not physics — a scoping decision, not a gap), and why medians, IQRs and worst cases are reported instead of single means.
  • All three rigs run constant, accelerated load. Real machines vary speed and load, which moves every feature with zero damage present. Nothing here tests that.

What's next

Dataset #4 will be my own rig.

The pipeline was built so a new dataset is a drop-in loader. The plan: a desktop run-to-failure rig around an SKF 6204 bearing with seeded defects — my own data, collected with the lessons above (fixed-duration healthy baseline, explicit stop criterion), graded by the same LOBO/LODO protocol.
